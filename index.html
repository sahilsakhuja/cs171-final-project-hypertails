What's Going On In Sudan?

Over the past 20 years, Sudan's economy has been in a rocky position, deteriorated firstly by the global financial crisis 2007-2008. Growth declined by 4% of GDP in 2009, from 10.2% in 2007 and 6% in 2008. Lack of foreign investments and slowing economic growth resulted in higher tax and scaled-up custom duties to generate revenues to cover government's expenses. Without the influx of foreign currency through foreign investments, the government had to draw on its foreign reserves and pegged its currency to the Euro. Sudanese pound has sharply depreciated against the USD while inflation rate rocketed.

The situation got worse following the aftermath of the ouster of former president in 2019 triggered by mass protest in the country.

Hyperinflation refers to rapid, excessive, and general price increases in an economy, typically more than 50% per month.

Hyperinflations are usually caused by rapid growth of money supply to cover for large government expenditures. It is not uncommon to see hyperinflations in developing countries such as Zimbabwe or Venezuela, yet it has never been an issue to developed countries such as US or UK.

However, since Covid-19, we have seen a huge disruption in supply chain as well as labor shortages that cause prices to increase sharply. According to the most recent data in October 2021, the US Consumer Price Index surged 6.2% from a year ago, the biggest inflation surge in 30 years. Fuel oil prices soared 12.3% in October 2021 while energy prices are up 30% for 12-month period. All these factors have a big impact on the current recovery process of the economy post-Covid 19, the currency value, and the future consumer spending.
